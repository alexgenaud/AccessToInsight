  • [30 October 1998]
    An anthology of all of Thanissaro Bhikkhu's sutta translations is now also available in Microsoft Word 6.0 (Macintosh/Windows) format. See the Theravada Text Archives page for more information.
  • [27 October 1998]
    The 1999 calendar of uposatha observance days is here.

    From the Anguttara Nikaya:

    • Anusota Sutta (AN 4.5) — With the Flow. A reminder that the popular advice to "just go with the flow" finds no support in the Buddha's teachings.
    • Agati Sutta (AN 4.19) — Off Course. The Buddha explains the difference between staying "on course" and straying "off course" in one's Dhamma practice.
    • Ahina Sutta (AN 4.67) — By a Snake. How the practice of metta (loving-kindness) can serve as a protection against harm.
    • Sappurisa Sutta (AN 4.73) — A Person of Integrity. Are you a person of integrity? The ways in which you speak about yourself and about others reveal much about your personal integrity.
    • Chavalata Sutta (AN 4.95) — The Firebrand. Which is better: to practice Dhamma for one's own benefit or for another's? The answer may surprise you.
    • Raga-vinaya Sutta (AN 4.96) — The Subduing of Passion. What does it mean, exactly, to practice Dhamma for one's own — or for another's — benefit?
    • Thana Sutta (AN 4.115) — Courses of Action. When faced with a choice, how does one decide which course of action to follow? The Buddha here offers some helpful advice.
    • Jambali Sutta (AN 4.178) — The Waste-water Pool. The Buddha uses some memorable similes to describe the overcoming of self-identification and ignorance.

  • [13 October 1998] From the Anguttara Nikaya:
    • Adhipateyya Sutta (AN 3.40) — Governing Principles. The Buddha describes three governing principles that keep one's Dhamma practice on-track. Beware: there's no place to hide from your unskillful actions!
    • Channa Sutta (AN 3.71) — To Channa the Wanderer. Ven. Ananda instructs Channa on how to abandon the mental defilements of passion, aversion, and delusion.
    • Sikkha Sutta (AN 3.88) — Trainings (1).
    • Sikkha Sutta (AN 3.89) — Trainings (2). The Buddha summarizes the three aspects of Dhamma practice that are to be developed.
    • Lonaphala Sutta (AN 3.99) — The Salt Crystal. Using several memorable similes, the Buddha explains why the consequences of unskillful deeds may appear to be severe for one person and mild for another. Moral: strengthen your virtue!
    • Pansadhovaka Sutta (AN 3.100 (i-x)) — The Dirt-washer.
    • Nimitta Sutta (AN 3.100 (xi-xv)) — Themes. In these two suttas the Buddha calls on us to train the mind with skill, purifying it as a goldsmith purifies gold ore.
